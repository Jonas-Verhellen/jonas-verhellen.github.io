Short-form Science

Welcome to my blog! Computational neuroscience, drug design, and severe mental disorders are three fascinating topics that intersect in interesting ways. Through the lens of computational neuroscience, we can gain a better understanding of the brain and its mechanisms, which can inform drug design for various conditions, including severe mental disorders. Exploring computational neuroscience and drug design can lead to important insights that can improve our understanding and treatment of these complex conditions.

The Genetic Architecture of Schizophrenia

Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) are used to determine the genetic architecture of polygenic traits such as schizophrenia and bipolar disorder. Common genetic variants found in these studies account for only a small percentage of the disease liability, with rare variants playing a potentially important role. Multiple genes implicated in schizophrenia target synaptic organization and function.

Open-Source: Molecular NSGA-II and NSGA-III

Traditional techniques such as graph-based genetic algorithms for molecular optimization have shown to be efficient and robust alternatives to data-driven deep generative models. Open-source implementations of two generations of graph-based non-dominated sorting genetic algorithms (NSGA-II, NSGA-III) have been provided for molecular multi-objective optimization highlighting the importance of chemical space exploration.

Argenomic: Illuminating Chemical Space

Argenomic is a chemical space exploration software that improves search efficiency compared to both machine learning and genetic algorithm approaches. It produces diverse high-performing molecules, illuminates the fitness of molecules over chemical space, and avoids stagnation by enforcing molecular diversity. Users can choose their own features of interest to explore and define relevant ranges of variation to construct a feature space.
